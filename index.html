Legendary Sports

A mobile app connecting adult users to recreational sports teams in their area

Project Summary

Legendary sports is an app designed to allow adult users to locate, communicate with, and join local recreation teams in their area. Working with a small design team, I conceptualized and designed a mobile app, including initial idea brainstorming, user interviews, wireframing, user testing, and building a high-fidelity prototype.

My Team

My team consisted of three other designers and myself.

My Roles

Project Manager

In my role as Project Manager, I supervised three other designers. We shared design responsibilities on this project, but I performed daily checkins, delegated tasks, and maintained our group Trello board.

US Researcher

As a UX Researcher, I worked with my team to develop interview and survey questions and drafted our research plan. I conducted one of the interviews we used to develop our user persona, as well as two of our user tests.

UX Designer

As UX Designer, I sketched one set of wireframes that we used to develop our low-fidelity wireframe. I created the overlays in our wireframe and checked all of our frames for symmetry, content, color, style, and interactions.

UX Writer

As UX Writer, I wrote the copy for much of our prototype, including the team information, event information, password reset dialog, and team announcements.

Design Process

Research

User Interviews

We conducted five one-on-one user interviews remotely via Zoom to collect qualitative data from participants. We specifically sought out individuals over the age of 18 who had some history of playing a team sport, since our app would be targeted specifically towards adults who were trying to find local rec teams outside of a professional, high school, or college setting.

Following our interviews, we synthesized our notes into an affinity diagram to chart and categorize the common trends in our interview responses.

User Survey

In addition to our user interviews, we created a survey on Google Forms using the same questions from our interviews. We distributed the survey to our class and on social media, including to a survey exchange group with more than 19,000 members. In total, we received 37 responses.

Competitor Analysis

We compared four different tools that connect their users to local sports to determine how their features match up to the wants and needs of our interviewees and survey respondents.

Definition

User Persona

Using the information gathered from our interviews and survey, we built a persona representing our ideal user. The persona helped guide us in developing the app by providing a clear image of the goals, needs, and frustrations we wanted to address.

Meet Michael

Our target user was Michael, a 25-year-old insurance agent from Minneapolis. Michael played sports in high school and college but hasn’t been able to find a team to connect with since graduating. He’d like to get back into recreational sports to make friends and stay in shape, but needs something that fits his schedule.

Michael cares a lot about knowing the vibe a group before joining, and wants to be on a team that matches his competitiveness and skill level.

The Big Question

We observed that there isn’t a reliable source of information on local, recreational sports teams geared towards adults. For this reason, it is difficult for adults to find recreational sports leagues to join where they can socialize and get physical activity.

“How might we help adults who are looking for a recreational sport find teams that are appropriate for their age and skill level, based on surveys of new players in local recreational leagues?”

Ideation

Journey Map

Having created our ideal user and identified our desired impact, our next step was to chart Michael’s experience using the app.

We outlined a general scenario for Michael and plotted the steps of his experience as he went from looking for a sport to join, to downloading Legendary Sports, to eventually connecting and chatting with a team.

We identified two key opportunities through our user journey map:

  • Building in a schedule feature
  • Having a chat function

Feature Prioritization Matrix

As we compiled the information from our affinity diagram, user survey, user persona, and journey map, we identified common patterns that we added to a feature prioritization matrix. Using the matrix, we identified key app features that would be most useful to users like Michael, including:

  • Searching by location
  • Filtering by skill level
  • Team calendars
  • Team messaging

To help make these features more functional, we added additional supporting features to our list:

  • Team announcements
  • Profile screens
  • Team rosters

User Flow

Once we identified our key features, the next step in our design process was to build a user flow mapping how Michael would move through Legendary Sports when using the app, from onboarding, to creating a profile, to finding and joining a team, to chatting with his new teammates and viewing his match schedule.

Prototyping

Sketching it Out

For our initial round of wireframes, each member of the design team sketched a set of frames. We then lined the frames up to match each step in the user flow, and then used dot voting to choose which sketch we preferred for each step on the flow. We then took the sketches, recreated them, and put them into a low-fi prototype on Invision.

Testing

Remote-Moderated User Tests

Using our low-fidelity prototype in Invision, we ran a round of remote-moderated user tests on three individuals.

All three of our users were able to successfully complete each of a list of three tasks with minimal questions or pain points. We received some feedback about navigation improvement and content, specifically that we needed to make buttons more prominent and make the purpose of each screen clearer.

Overall, the feedback we received was largely positive. Users navigated the app easily, didn’t have any hard or confusing decisions, liked the flow, and enjoyed the idea of the app.

We took the constructive feedback received during user testing and plotted it on a priority matrix to help determine how best to iterate our design.

Iteration

Wireframe Iteration

After user testing, we updated our user flow to adjust some minor navigation concerns. With the updated user flow and our user feedback as guides, we iterated our original wireframes and moved from low-fidelity to high-fidelity. We also chose to switch from an iOS style to using the Material Design Guidelines after receiving feedback that our design more closely matched themes common to the Material Design Guidelines.

Signup Iteration

We added our logo to the signup screen, as well as password confirmation input. We changed our social media login from text buttons to round icon buttons.

Team Landing Screen

We focused on maximizing space for graphics on the team page, with a large “Join Team” button and a small “Upcoming Events” tool that opens the team calendar.

Team Information Screen

We split the team information screen into quadrants with icons, rather than having a text-heavy team page. We also moved the “Message Team” button to the top of the team page.

Design Style

As a team, we conceptualized a design style that we applied to our prototype. Our focus was on usability and visual appeal, with some inspiration from existing athletics-inspired styles.

Typography

We decided to use Lobster in our logo for its bold, artistic style. Because of its booming popularity a decade ago, the font may remind target users of clubs, teams, businesses, and events from the last time they were able to play on a team. That nostalgia could be a solid starting point for catching the eye of a user like Michael’s.

Our headings, buttons, and body text use Roboto, a strong sans serif font that is clean and readable.

Color Palette

Our color palette was inspired by blue and orange, one of the most popular color schemes in sports. Blue and orange is used by teams like the Denver Broncos, Chicago Bears, and New York Knicks and Mets.

We believed that featuring our color palette in our icons would also make them stand out against a neutral background.

Conclusion

Prototype

Final Presentation

At the end of our design studio, we presented our work via Zoom to six other design teams and three senior designers.

Although most of the feedback we received was related to our presentation slides and our presentation style in general, the feedback related to our app design was very positive, including that our research was very strong and that we excelled at applying user feedback in our iterations.

We received some suggestions for future development, including:

  • Including some team management functions in our design.
  • Iterating our logo design and app name to better relate to the application.

Looking Back

Having completed this design studio, there are some specific things we would have done differently:

  • Due to some miscommunication on our design team between whether we were designing for iOS or using the Material Design Guidelines, our app’s overall design rests somewhere between the two. Improving overall communication would have avoided this.
  • Our design process would have been more efficient had we built a component library. Although our design is consistent across screens, having to recreate design elements slowed down the overall design process.
  • Scope creep was a definite issue for our team in this project. Given our limited time in our first design studio, it would have been better to focus on fewer features to maximize design consistency and to more thoroughly target our research.

Looking Forward

In the future, we hope to iterate on this design in a number of specific ways, including:

  • Developing user flows for different types of users, such as coaches, team administrators, and fans.
  • Researching and implementing a better way for users to join teams, rather than simply being automatically admitted.
  • Designing a more interactive calendar to show practices and game dates.
  • Including more features for showing league standings, tournament brackets, and player highlights.
  • Designing a way for users to connect with their friends on the app.
  • Designing a way for users to rate teams, similar to yelp reviews for teams.
